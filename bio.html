Music Career

1960s–1970s Balasubrahmanyam and his wife being congratulated by K. J. Kodandapani. The first non-Telugu song that he recorded was in Kannada in 1966 for the film Nakkare Ade Swarga, starring T. R. Narasimharaju. He recorded his first Tamil song "Athaanodu Ippadi Irundhu Eththanai Naalaachu", a duet with L. R. Eswari under the musical direction of M. S. Viswanathan for the film Hotel Ramba, which never released. The song "Medante Meda Kaadu" from the film Sukha Dukhalu (1968) composed by Kondandapani brought him recognition in Telugu cinema. Other early songs he sang were duets with P. Susheela, "Iyarkai Ennum Ilaya Kanni" in the 1969 film Shanti Nilayam, starring Gemini Ganesan, and "Aayiram Nilavae Vaa" for M. G. Ramachandran in Adimaippenn. His first song with S. Janaki was "Pournami Nilavil Pani Vizhum Iravil" in Kanni Penn. He was then introduced to the Malayalam film industry by G. Devarajan in the film Kadalppalam. As per Balasubrahmanyam, "Ye Divilo Virisina Parijathamo" from the 1973 Telugu film Kannevayasu composed by Satyam gave an amazing turning point to his singing career. Balasubrahmanyam recorded the most songs on a single day by any singer.He had recorded 21 songs in Kannada for the composer Upendra Kumar in Bangalore from 9:00 am to 9:00 pm on 8 February 1981 and 19 songs in Tamil, 16 songs in Hindi in a day.[50] In his words, "There were days when I used to record 15–20 songs, but only for Anand Milind. And I would take the last flight back to Chennai." In the 1970s, he also worked with M. S. Viswanathan in Tamil movies for actors such as M. G. Ramachandran, Sivaji Ganesan and Gemini Ganesan. He recorded duets with P. Susheela, S. Janaki, Vani Jayaram and L. R. Eswari. Balasubrahmanyam's association with Ilaiyaraaja began even before Ilaiyaraaja came to the cine field. In the early days, he used to sing in towns and villages all over south India and Ilaiyaraaja, then an unknown harmonium and guitar player accompanied him in his concerts. 1980s Balasubrahmanyam gained international recognition with the 1980 film Sankarabharanam. The film is considered to be one of the best films ever to emerge from the Telugu film industry. Directed by K. Viswanath, the film's soundtrack was composed by K. V. Mahadevan and led to an increase in the usage of Carnatic music in Telugu cinema as well as in other languages. Not a classically trained singer, he used a "film music" aesthetic in recording the songs. Balasubrahmanyam received his first National Film Award for Best Male Playback Singer for his work. His first work in Hindi films was in the following year, in Ek Duuje Ke Liye (1981),for which he received another National Film Award for Best Male Playback Singer. Balasubrahmanyam began to record more songs in Telugu and other South Indian languages, especially for Ilaiyaraaja with S. Janaki, the trio considered to be highly successful in the Tamil film industry from the late 1970s and throughout the 1980s. The songs were based on classical music, such as in Saagara Sangamam (1983) and Rudraveena (1988), for which Ilaiyaraaja and Balasubrahmanyam won National Awards for Best Music Director and Best Male Playback Singer respectively. South Indian actor Chiranjeevi said that SPB's voice is one of the main reasons for his success. In 1989, Balasubrahmanyam was the playback singer for actor Salman Khan in Maine Pyar Kiya. The soundtrack for the film won a Filmfare Award for Best Male Playback Singer for the song Dil Deewana. For much of the next decade, Balasubrahmanyam continued as the "romantic singing voice" on the soundtracks of Khan's films. Notable among these was Hum Aapke Hain Koun..! which became the highest-grossing Hindi film of all time. Balasubrahmanyam's duet with Lata Mangeshkar, "Didi Tera Devar Deewana", was very popular. Education

Born on 4 June 1946 in Konetammapeta, Nellore district, Andhra Pradesh, SPB grew up in a musically inclined Telugu Brahmin family. His father, S. P. Sambamurthy, was a renowned Harikatha exponent, nurturing SPB’s early musical exposure.Following his father’s wishes, he enrolled at JNTU College of Engineering in Anantapur to study Mechanical Engineering.However, a bout of typhoid forced him to abandon his engineering degree and motivated him to explore alternative paths. While his formal academic journey ended, SPB never halted his learning: he won a major singing competition in Chennai in 1964, judged by S. P. Kodandapani and Ghantasala, which transformed his pursuit from hobby to profession.Self‑taught, he mastered notation and language versatility—recording in Telugu, Tamil, Kannada, Hindi, Malayalam, Sanskrit, English, Urdu, and other dialects . During college, he formed a light‑music troupe with Ilaiyaraaja, Anirutta, Gangai Amaran, and others, performing and honing stagecraft.Though never formally trained in classical music, he achieved mastery through self‑discipline, analytical listening, and methodical preparation—understanding lyrics deeply before performance . Balancing engineering constraints and musical inclination, he charted a unique path that melded technical rigor with creative finesse. SPB remained academically engaged by joining Chennai’s Institution of Engineers as an associate member after discontinuing studies . His education may have ended prematurely, but it shaped his inquisitive spirit that fueled lifelong learning and musical excellence.

Personal Life

S. P. Balasubrahmanyam (SPB) married his distant relative Savitri in 1969 at the Simhachalam Temple in Visakhapatnam—defying initial familial objections due to sharing the same gothra. Their love story is often remembered as one of traditional yet courageous romance.Though they became a renowned couple in the public eye, SPB and Savitri chose to keep their personal world largely private. They remained devoted to family life, rooted in Telugu Brahmin culture, faith, and community ties . From early on, SPB was deeply influenced by his mentor S. P. Kodandapani, crediting him as his guru. He even named his theater and production company after Kodandapani, carrying forward his legacy with profound respect.SPB’s humility and kindness were legendary. In a 2009 interview, a journalist shared how SPB, despite being late for an appointment, warmly invited her into his home, offered breakfast (upma!), even adjusting recipes to match her tastes, and chatted in her native Kannada—all to put her at ease . He’d often say, “If you want to learn, you can also learn from children,” embodying not just a warm fatherly role, but also as a mentor on TV shows like Super Singer Junior, where his gentle encouragement resonated with many.SPB enjoyed everyday pleasures: he loved ice cream, smoked early on (though later quit cigarettes), and occasionally drank alcohol—openly admitting he never followed voice-restricting diets. He’d often say, “I like to eat ice creams a lot and it has never affected my voice”. Deeply spiritual, he supported charitable causes via the SP Sambamurthy Foundation—named for his father—to help the underprivileged. He was also known for sponsoring chess tournaments, including maestro Viswanathan Anand’s team in 1983. SPB built a reputation as a conscientious board member in the Music Singers Association—attending meetings always early, offering heartfelt gratitude for contributions, and exchanging notes with senior artists. He stayed emotionally connected with fans—even those far away. In a touching gesture, upon hearing from a Sri Lankan professor who credited SPB’s songs with saving his life, SPB made a surprise visit to meet him personally. When COVID‑19 struck in 2020, his family and fans were deeply involved in supporting him. His son, S. P. Charan, posted regular health updates on social media and thanked fans for their prayers, saying “SPB belongs to everyone.” Even in the ICU, SPB calmly assured people, “I am fine,” keeping everyone hopeful .

Achievements

SPB’s achievements are a testament to his unparalleled talent and dedication. He received six National Film Awards in Best Male Playback Singer categories across Hindi, Telugu, Tamil, and Kannada languages.He was awarded the prestigious Padma Shri (2001) and Padma Bhushan (2011) for his impact on Indian arts, and posthumously honoured with the Padma Vibhushan (2021).Within Telugu cinema, he claimed more than 25 Nandi Awards, also drawing accolades from Karnataka and Tamil Nadu state film industries.He won multiple Filmfare Awards South and a Hindi Filmfare Award for Maine Pyar Kiya.At the 47th IFFI in 2016, he received the Centenary Award for Indian Film Personality of the Year . His name is recorded in the Guinness Book for the most recorded songs by a singer—over 40,000 to 50,000 tracks.He achieved remarkable studio feats, including recording 21 Kannada songs in 12 hours, and 19 Tamil and 16 Hindi songs in single days .Additional tributes include the NTR National Award (2012), Harivarasanam Award (2015), and FilmFans Association honors . Beyond singing, he thrived as an actor in ~40 films, a music director for ~45 films, a dubbing artist, and a beloved television presenter . His awards tally exceeds 125 wins, spanning civilian, state, national, and industry accolades—a truly monumental legacy .Main article: List of awards and nominations received by S. P. Balasubrahmanyam Throughout his career, Balasubrahmanyam has won awards not only for playback singing, but also for music direction, acting, dubbing and producing. He has won six National Film Awards for Best Male Playback Singer. He was also awarded the Padma Shri in 2001 and the Padma Bhushan in 2011 and Padma Vibhushan in 2021 (Posthumously).

Children-life

S. P. Balasubrahmanyam and Savitri had two children who carry forward his legacy. Their daughter, Pallavi, inherited his passion for singing, making her presence known through performances and independent releases.Their son, S. P. Charan, pursued playback singing and became an actor and film producer in Telugu and Tamil film industries. SPB often expressed that he learned as much from his children as they did from him, highlighting a bond of mutual respect . During his illness in 2020, Charan openly shared health updates on social media, thanking fans and reinforcing SPB’s connection with the public . The family remained united throughout his health crisis; both children were present by his side, managing public communications with grace . Following his passing, Charan’s heartfelt words—“SPB belongs to everyone”—reaffirmed his father’s universal appeal and humility. Pallavi and Charan continue to honor his memory through music, maintaining the family’s tradition of spreading joy through song.S. P. Balasubrahmanyam had two children—S. P. Charan, a well-known figure in the South Indian entertainment industry, and Pallavi Balasubrahmanyam, who has maintained a lower profile but shares her father’s musical talent. Charan, born in 1972, followed in his father's footsteps and became a playback singer, actor, and producer. Educated in the U.S. with a degree in Business Administration from Pace University, he returned to India to pursue a career in music and film. His singing credits include several hit Telugu and Tamil songs, and he gained recognition for producing successful films like Chennai 600028 and Aaranya Kaandam—the latter winning a National Film Award. Besides being a respected producer, Charan has also acted in films and participated in television music shows, including serving as a judge. He has been candid about living under the shadow of his legendary father, but over the years, carved his own space in the industry. His personal life included a first marriage that ended in divorce and a second marriage to Aparna in 2012. During SPB’s illness in 2020, Charan became the face of the family, regularly updating the public on his father’s condition and showing grace under pressure. Pallavi, on the other hand, has largely stayed out of the limelight. Though not actively involved in the mainstream film industry, she has displayed her musical skills through occasional classical and devotional performances. She is known to be deeply spiritual and prefers a quiet life away from public attention. Both children were extremely close to their father, and after his death, they have continued to honor his legacy—Charan more publicly through tributes, music, and managing his father’s unreleased works, while Pallavi does so quietly through personal remembrance. Together, they represent two sides of their father’s legacy: one outward-facing and influential in the entertainment world, and the other more introspective and spiritually grounded.

Illness and Death

On 5 August 2020, Balasubrahmanyam tested positive for COVID-19 during the COVID-19 pandemic in India and was admitted to MGM Healthcare in Chennai. Subsequently, his health deteriorated and he was shifted to the intensive care unit in a critical state. He required a ventilator and extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) support. It is stated that it was due to his age (74 years) and multiple comorbidities he had (like diabetes) that his condition worsened. His son Charan provided periodic updates over social media, while members of the Tamil film industry engaged in a mass prayer via Zoom on 20 August and candlelight vigils were held by fans outside the hospital. Similarly many actors of Telugu film industry urged people to pray for his life, many Tollywood music composers also organised virtual mass prayers. Members of the Kannada film industry got together for another mass prayer on 4 September 2020 in Bengaluru. On 7 September 2020, Balasubrahmanyam tested negative for the coronavirus, although he remained using a ventilator and ECMO. He started showing signs of recovery, including light speech and physical activities. However, the hospital released a statement on 24 September stating that he became "extremely critical" and was on "maximal life support". Balasubrahmanyam died on 25 September 2020 at 1:04 pm (IST) of cardio-respiratory arrest after over a month-long hospitalisation. He was buried at his farm house in Thamaraipaakkam, Thiruvallur district with state honours on 26 September 2020.
